Neuro-Symbolic Mixture of Experts with Cognitive Knowledge Graphs for Scholarly Knowledge Organization

Gollam Rabby2◦, Parvez Zamil1, Sören Auer1,2,
TIB—Leibniz Information Centre for Science and Technology, Hannover, Germany1, L3S Research Center, Leibniz University Hannover, Hannover, Germany 2
Dataset Code arXiv

Abstract

The publication flood with more and more scholarly articles published every year increasingly hampers scientific progress. This research introduces a Neuro-Symbolic Mixture of Experts (NS-MoE) system for Open Research Knowledge Graph (ORKG) that synergistically combines symbolic and neural models to categorize scholarly articles and articulate their contributions within a structured Cognitive Knowledge Graph (CKG). Unlike prior approaches limited to either symbolic or neural paradigms within specific domains, NS-MoE captures both domain-specific and domain-independent knowledge, facilitating comprehensive and comparable scholarly representations. The system enables the generation of structured contribution descriptions as CKGs, offering customizable pathways that integrate symbolic reasoning with neural inference. By leveraging smaller, efficient neural models, this approach addresses concerns related to computational resources, cost-effectiveness, and environmental impact. Furthermore, the incorporation of domain-expert-verified scholarly data enhances the accuracy and reliability of the neural models. Empirical evaluations demonstrate the efficacy of the NS-MoE system, achieving an accuracy of 94% for research field classification and an F1 score of 71% for predicate label prediction using statistical methods. Agent-based evaluations yielded 91% accuracy for research field classification and a 74% F1 score for predicate label prediction. These results surpass the performance of existing pre-trained or fine-tuned expert neural models. The integration of this system into the ORKG exemplifies its practical applicability, facilitating precise access to organized scholarly knowledge in a cost-effective and resource-efficient manner. This work under scores the potential of neuro-symbolic approaches in enhancing the structuring and dissemination of scholarly information.
